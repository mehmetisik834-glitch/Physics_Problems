# Physics Analysis: Energy Dissipation in a Bouncing Ball

This analysis calculates the height of a tennis ball after multiple bounces, accounting for a fixed percentage of energy loss per impact.

## 1. Key Definitions & Formulas

### Gravitational Potential Energy ($U$)
When an object is at a height $h$, its energy is stored as potential energy. In this problem, we assume the ball is momentarily at rest at its peak height, so its total mechanical energy equals its potential energy:

$$
U = mgh
$$

Where:
* **$m$:** Mass of the ball.
* **$g$:** Acceleration due to gravity.
* **$h$:** Height above the ground.

### Energy Retention Factor ($r$)
If a ball loses a percentage of energy, the energy remaining (retained) is:

$$
E_{after} = E_{before} \times (1 - \text{loss rate})
$$

In this case, a 30% loss means the ball retains **70%** (or $0.7$) of its energy.

### Relationship between Energy and Height
Since $U \propto h$ (energy is directly proportional to height), the height after a bounce is directly proportional to the energy remaining:

$$
h_{new} = r \cdot h_{old}
$$

---

## 2. Problem Solving

**Given Data:**
* Initial height ($h_0$): $2.0 \text{ m}$
* Energy loss per bounce: $30\%$
* Energy retention factor ($r$): $0.7$

### Step 1: Height after the First Bounce ($h_1$)
The ball loses 30% of its energy upon the first impact. Therefore, it will rise to 70% of its previous height:

$$
h_1 = h_0 \times 0.7
$$

$$
h_1 = 2.0 \times 0.7 = 1.4 \text{ m}
$$

### Step 2: Height after the Second Bounce ($h_2$)
The ball hits the ground again, starting from $h_1$. It loses another 30% of its current mechanical energy:

$$
h_2 = h_1 \times 0.7
$$

$$
h_2 = 1.4 \times 0.7
$$

$$
h_2 = 0.98 \text{ m}
$$

---

## 3. Alternative Method: The Power Law
For any number of bounces ($n$), the height can be calculated using the formula:

$$
h_n = h_0 \cdot r^n
$$

For the second bounce ($n=2$):

$$
h_2 = 2.0 \cdot (0.7)^2
$$

$$
h_2 = 2.0 \cdot 0.49 = 0.98 \text{ m}
$$

---

## 4. Conclusion

After the second bounce, the tennis ball will rise to a height of **$0.98 \text{ meters}$**.

**Note on Energy Dissipation:**
The "lost" 30% of energy is not destroyed but transformed into other forms, primarily:
* **Thermal Energy:** Heat generated by the deformation of the ball and the floor.
* **Acoustic Energy:** The "pop" sound heard during the bounce.
* **Elastic Internal Energy:** Work done during the compression of the rubber.
