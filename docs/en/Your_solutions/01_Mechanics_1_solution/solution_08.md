## 8. Centripetal Acceleration at the Equator

### Problem Statement
Calculate the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) of a person standing on the Earth's equator.
- **Radius ($r$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6.378 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$
- **Period ($T$):** $24 \text{ hours} = 86,400 \text{ s}$

### 1. Angular Velocity ($\omega$)
The angular velocity of Earth's rotation is:

$$
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = \frac{2\pi}{86,400} \approx 7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s}
$$

### 2. Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
Using the relation $a_c = \omega^2 r$:

$$
a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5})^2 \cdot (6.378 \times 10^6)
$$

$$
a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2
$$

### 3. Conclusion
The centripetal acceleration at the equator is approximately **$0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$**. This value is roughly $0.34\%$ of the acceleration due to gravity ($g$), explaining why effective gravity is slightly lower at the equator than at the poles.
